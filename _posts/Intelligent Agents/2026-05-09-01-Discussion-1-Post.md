Agent-based systems are attractive solutions in situations where a centralized system would be unable to access the information required to make a decision in time, or would otherwise be incapable of handling the complexity of the task (Marik and McFarlane, 2005). By coordinating local decisions in the service of a global goal, intelligent agents can achieve much higher robustness in the face of a rapidly changing environment, as compared to conventional hierarchical system (Marik and McFarlane, 2005). However, this can result in systems that are difficult to simulate (Leitão, Mařík and Vrba, 2013) and produce unpredictable behaviour at the top level (Marik and McFarlane, 2005).

I have some concerns about the role LLMs with play in the next generation of intelligent agents. On one hand, deep learning approaches bring a level of flexibility and learning that previous attempts using ontologies or handwritten rules cannot match (An, 2026). On the other hand, the technology with which they are implemented is still immature and there are multiple competing approaches, none of which completely solve the problems earlier agent architectures encountered. For example, LLMs act upon the world by selecting tools through free-form text, which requires the agent to have perfect formatting, or else the action can fail (An, 2026). The result is that the previous issue of non-determinism, which only existed at the global level, now exists at the local level as well. At first LLMs appear as though they are a jump forward for social agents. However, they are susceptible to over-communicating, resulting in context explosion (An, 2026). This challenge of balancing keeping knowledge fresh with reducing message volume and size was already a crucial research topic over two decades ago (Marik and McFarlane, 2005). What’s next? Are social agent architectures built around LLMs the future or a fad?
