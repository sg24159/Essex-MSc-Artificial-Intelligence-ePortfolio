I’m one of the people who got on the OpenClaw bandwagon. I’ve since switched to Nanobot, but the concept is the same, an agent designed to autonomously perform chains of actions on your behalf (nanobot - Ultra-Lightweight Personal AI Agent, no date; OpenClaw — Personal AI Assistant, no date). Giving an agent access to your accounts, and allowing it to run autonomously, requires some serious thought about constraints.

Historically, safety research has focused on alignment, and more recently, output moderation and simple guardrails. However, while necessary, those alone are not sufficient for safe and effective control with modern LLM based agents (Li, Feng and Sun, 2026). An aligned agent may attempt helpful, but unsafe, actions, such as using found credentials, or deleting barriers that have good reason for existing (John Hughes, 2026).

The best guardrail would be a human thoroughly reviewing each agent action, however, this is not practical in practice, and quickly leads to approval fatigue. One solution for this problem of bounded autonomy is to have a second agent whose sole purpose is classifying actions as unsafe or not, alerting a human supervisor with an approval request if it’s not sure (John Hughes, 2026). This is similar to the vertical architecture described by (Russell and Norvig, 2021) in Artificial Intelligence: a Modern Approach; a lower layer could be optimized for autonomy and exploration, while higher layers monitor safety.

Multi-agent architectures, as I noted in my first discussion post, can be harder to predict, and therefore harder to constrain (Marik and McFarlane, 2005; Leitão, Mařík and Vrba, 2013). In my development project, we used multiple agents, each with a very narrow scope and one-way communication. This reduced the range of possible safety violations to almost nothing, although it did make the system more fragile and less powerful.
